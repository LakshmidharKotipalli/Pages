Why RAG Exists

LLMs are brilliant — but frozen in time

Large Language Models know a tremendous amount. But they have a major weakness: their knowledge is baked in at training time and can't be updated without retraining. RAG solves this — and much more.

📅
Knowledge Cutoff
LLMs are trained on data up to a certain date. They don't know about events, documents, or changes that happened after that cutoff.
💭
Hallucinations
Without access to real facts, LLMs sometimes "make up" plausible-sounding but incorrect information — confidently and convincingly.
🏢
Private Data Blind Spot
Your company's internal documents, databases, and knowledge bases were never part of the model's training. The LLM simply doesn't know them.
📎
No Source Citations
A base LLM can't tell you where it got its information from. With RAG, every answer can be traced back to a specific source document.

Indexing Phase — Step 1
The first step is gathering all your knowledge. RAG can ingest many document types: PDFs, Word files, HTML web pages, Markdown files, database records, Notion pages, Confluence wikis, CSVs, and more.

Document loaders (like those in LangChain or LlamaIndex) handle parsing — extracting raw text and preserving metadata like file name, page number, author, and last-modified date. This metadata travels alongside every chunk and is crucial for source attribution and filtering later.
Analogy: Think of this like gathering every book, report, and sticky note in a company's office before building an index. The richer and more diverse your document sources, the more comprehensive your RAG system will be.

Step 3 of Indexing

Embedding Models

Embeddings are the magic behind semantic search. They convert text into lists of numbers — vectors — that capture the meaning of the text. Semantically similar texts end up with similar vectors, which means they'll be physically close in vector space.

What is an Embedding?

Imagine a giant coordinate system with 1,536 dimensions (or more). Every piece of text gets placed at a specific point in that space. The model is trained so that texts with similar meaning land near each other, and texts with different meanings land far apart.

For example, "dog" and "puppy" will be very close together. "dog" and "quantum physics" will be far apart. This geometric distance is what makes semantic search possible.

Analogy: Think of embeddings as GPS coordinates for meaning. Just as two cities on the same coast have similar latitude/longitude coordinates, two texts about the same topic have similar embedding coordinates.

How Similarity Works — Cosine Similarity

To compare two embeddings, we measure the cosine similarity — the cosine of the angle between the two vectors. A score of 1.0 means identical direction (very similar), and 0.0 means perpendicular (unrelated).

The Golden Rule of Embedding Models

You MUST use the same embedding model at index time and query time. If you embed your documents with OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large, you must also embed every user query with text-embedding-3-large. Different models produce vectors in incompatible spaces — mixing them will return completely random results.

Step 4 of Indexing

Vector Databases

A vector database is purpose-built to store embeddings and perform lightning-fast similarity searches. Traditional databases search by exact matches; vector databases search by semantic closeness — finding the nearest neighbors in high-dimensional space.

Why Not Just Use a Regular Database?

PostgreSQL can store a list of 1,536 numbers. But scanning all rows and computing cosine similarity between your query vector and every stored vector is O(n) — it gets slow fast. With millions of chunks, this is impractical.

Vector databases use Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) algorithms that trade a tiny bit of accuracy for massive speed gains — searching millions of vectors in milliseconds.

Analogy: Finding a friend in New York City. A brute-force search means checking every person in the city. ANN is like saying "they live in Manhattan, probably near Central Park" — you jump to the right neighborhood first, then search locally.

Exact vs Approximate Search

Brute Force (Exact)

Compare query against every single vector. Guaranteed perfect results.

O(n·d) time 100% recall Slow at scale

ANN (Approximate)

Smart indexing structures to skip most comparisons. Near-perfect results, massively faster.

O(log n) time 95-99% recall Milliseconds

HNSW — How the Most Popular Index Works

Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) is the indexing algorithm used by most major vector databases. It builds a layered graph of nodes. Higher layers have fewer nodes (long-range connections), lower layers have all nodes (short-range connections). Click "Step Through" to watch the search process.

Layer: —
Click "Step Through Search" to begin. HNSW starts at the top layer (fewest nodes) and works down, zooming into the answer like a map going from country → city → street.

Indexing Strategies Compared

HNSW — Hierarchical Navigable Small World

Builds a multi-layer proximity graph. Each node connects to its nearest neighbors. The top layer has a small subset of nodes with long-range links (like highways). Lower layers add more nodes with shorter-range links (like local streets). Search starts at the top layer and greedily descends.

PROS
  • ✓Fastest query speed in practice
  • ✓Very high recall (95-99%)
  • ✓Supports incremental inserts
CONS
  • ✗High RAM usage (full index in memory)
  • ✗Slow to build for huge datasets
Key params: M (connections per node, typically 16–64), ef_construction (build quality), ef_search (search quality). Higher = better recall but more memory/time.

IVF — Inverted File Index

Clusters all vectors into Voronoi regions using k-means (imagine partitioning the vector space into roughly equal cells). When a query arrives, only the closest nprobe cluster centers are searched, skipping most of the data.

IVF Flat stores raw vectors in each cluster (no compression). Good recall but high memory. Often combined with Product Quantization (IVFPQ) for compression.

Key params: nlist (number of clusters — typically √n to 4√n), nprobe (clusters to search at query time — higher = better recall, slower).

PQ / IVFPQ — Product Quantization

Compresses vectors by splitting each high-dimensional vector into sub-vectors and quantizing each sub-vector to a codebook of M centroids. A 1,536-dim float32 vector (~6KB) can be compressed to just ~64 bytes — a 100x reduction.

IVFPQ combines IVF partitioning with PQ compression. This enables storing hundreds of millions of vectors in RAM at the cost of some recall (~80-95%).

Best for: Very large datasets (100M+ vectors) where memory is constrained. Common in FAISS-based systems.

Flat Index — Exact Brute Force

No indexing tricks — just stores raw vectors and computes exact cosine/dot-product similarity against all of them at query time. Guarantees 100% recall and perfect results.

Surprisingly usable up to ~100K vectors with modern hardware. Simple to reason about and great for prototyping or small datasets. ChromaDB uses this by default for small collections.

Best for: Prototyping, small datasets (<100K vectors), or cases where 100% recall is non-negotiable and speed isn't critical.

Interactive: Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)

When using hybrid retrieval, you get two ranked lists — one from BM25, one from dense search. RRF fuses them into a single ranked list. The formula is: RRF(d) = Σ 1/(k + rank(d)) where k=60 is a constant. Documents that rank high in multiple lists get promoted. Click "Animate Fusion" to see it in action.

# ── Full RAG Indexing Pipeline ──────────────────────────────
from langchain.document_loaders import PyPDFLoader, DirectoryLoader
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings
from qdrant_client import QdrantClient
from qdrant_client.models import Distance, VectorParams

# 1. LOAD DOCUMENTS ──────────────────────────────────────────
loader = DirectoryLoader("./docs", glob="**/*.pdf", loader_cls=PyPDFLoader)
documents = loader.load()
print(ff"Loaded {len(documents)} document pages")

# 2. CHUNK TEXT ──────────────────────────────────────────────
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=512,       # target characters per chunk
    chunk_overlap=50,     # overlap to preserve context
    length_function=len,
    # tries these separators in order:
    separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " ", ""],
)
chunks = splitter.split_documents(documents)
print(ff"Split into {len(chunks)} chunks")

# 3. EMBED CHUNKS ────────────────────────────────────────────
embedding_model = OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small")
# Embed in batches to avoid rate limits
texts = [chunk.page_content for chunk in chunks]
embeddings = embedding_model.embed_documents(texts)  # List[List[float]]
print(ff"Each embedding has {len(embeddings[0])} dimensions")

# 4. STORE IN VECTOR DATABASE ────────────────────────────────
client = QdrantClient(url="http://localhost:6333")

# Create collection with HNSW index
client.create_collection(
    collection_name="my_knowledge_base",
    vectors_config=VectorParams(
        size=1536,              # match embedding model dimensions
        distance=Distance.COSINE,
    ),
)

# Upsert all chunks with their embeddings + metadata
from qdrant_client.models import PointStruct
points = [
    PointStruct(
        id=i,
        vector=embeddings[i],
        payload={
            "text": chunks[i].page_content,
            "source": chunks[i].metadata["source"],
            "page": chunks[i].metadata.get("page", 0),
        },
    )
    for i in range(len(chunks))
]
client.upsert(collection_name="my_knowledge_base", points=points)
print("Indexing complete!")

from openai import OpenAI
client = OpenAI()

response = client.embeddings.create(
    input=["Hello world", "Hi there"],
    model="text-embedding-3-small",
)
v1 = response.data[0].embedding  # list of 1536 floats
v2 = response.data[1].embedding

# ── Open-Source (HuggingFace sentence-transformers) ───────────
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
model = SentenceTransformer("BAAI/bge-m3")
embeddings = model.encode(["text 1", "text 2"], normalize_embeddings=True)

# ── Cosine Similarity ─────────────────────────────────────────
def cosine_similarity(a, b):
    a, b = np.array(a), np.array(b)
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

score = cosine_similarity(v1, v2)
print(ff"Similarity: {score:.4f}")  # e.g. 0.9432

# ── Dimensionality Reduction (OpenAI text-embedding-3 trick) ──
# text-embedding-3 supports truncation — you can request fewer dims!
response_small = client.embeddings.create(
    input=["Hello"],
    model="text-embedding-3-large",
    dimensions=512,  # reduce from 3072 to 512 — saves storage!

Python — Qdrant Vector DB
from qdrant_client import QdrantClient, models

client = QdrantClient(url="http://localhost:6333")

# ── Query / Retrieve ─────────────────────────────────────────
def retrieve(query_text: str, top_k: int = 5):
    # 1. Embed the query
    query_vector = embedding_model.embed_query(query_text)

    # 2. Similarity search
    results = client.query_points(
        collection_name="my_knowledge_base",
        query=query_vector,
        limit=top_k,
        with_payload=True,
        score_threshold=0.6,  # only return if similarity > 0.6
    )
    return results.points

# ── Filtered Search ─────────────────────────────────────────
def retrieve_from_source(query_text, source_filter, top_k=5):
    query_vector = embedding_model.embed_query(query_text)
    results = client.query_points(
        collection_name="my_knowledge_base",
        query=query_vector,
        query_filter=models.Filter(
            must=[models.FieldCondition(
                key="source",
                match=models.MatchValue(value=source_filter),
            )]
        ),
        limit=top_k,
    )
    return results.points

# ── Generate answer with retrieved context ───────────────────
def rag_answer(question: str) -> str:
    chunks = retrieve(question)
    context = "\n\n".join([c.payload["text"] for c in chunks])
    sources = [c.payload["source"] for c in chunks]

    prompt = f"""Answer the question using ONLY the context below.
If unsure, say "I don't have enough information."

Context:
{context}

Question: {question}
Answer:"""

    response = openai_client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
    )
    answer = response.choices[0].message.content
    return {"answer": answer, "sources": sources}
Python — Hybrid Search + RRF
from rank_bm25 import BM25Okapi
import numpy as np

# ── BM25 Index ───────────────────────────────────────────────
corpus = [chunk.page_content for chunk in chunks]
tokenized = [doc.lower().split() for doc in corpus]
bm25 = BM25Okapi(tokenized)

def hybrid_retrieve(query: str, top_k: int = 10, rrf_k: int = 60):
    # ── Dense retrieval ────────────────────────────────────────
    q_vec = embedding_model.embed_query(query)
    dense_results = client.query_points(
        collection_name="my_knowledge_base",
        query=q_vec, limit=top_k * 2,
    ).points
    dense_ids = [r.id for r in dense_results]

    # ── Sparse retrieval (BM25) ────────────────────────────────
    tokenized_query = query.lower().split()
    bm25_scores = bm25.get_scores(tokenized_query)
    bm25_ids = np.argsort(bm25_scores)[::-1][:top_k * 2].tolist()

    # ── Reciprocal Rank Fusion ────────────────────────────────
    rrf_scores = {}
    for rank, doc_id in enumerate(dense_ids):
        rrf_scores[doc_id] = rrf_scores.get(doc_id, 0) + 1 / (rrf_k + rank + 1)
    for rank, doc_id in enumerate(bm25_ids):
        rrf_scores[doc_id] = rrf_scores.get(doc_id, 0) + 1 / (rrf_k + rank + 1)

    # Sort by combined RRF score and return top-k
    fused = sorted(rrf_scores.items(), key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return [corpus[doc_id] for doc_id, _ in fused[:top_k]]
Python — HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings)
# HyDE: Instead of embedding the question, embed a hypothetical answer
# This bridges the gap between short questions and long document chunks

def hyde_retrieve(question: str, top_k: int = 5):
    # Step 1: Generate a hypothetical answer using the LLM
    # (This is just a guess — accuracy doesn't matter much here)
    hyde_prompt = f"""Write a detailed paragraph that would answer the following question.
Focus on being informative and relevant, even if you're not sure of exact facts.

Question: {question}

Hypothetical Answer:"""

    hyp_answer = openai_client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o-mini",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": hyde_prompt}],
        max_tokens=200,
    ).choices[0].message.content

    # Step 2: Embed the hypothetical answer (NOT the original question)
    hyp_vector = embedding_model.embed_query(hyp_answer)

    # Step 3: Use hypothetical vector to search the real document store
    results = client.query_points(
        collection_name="my_knowledge_base",
        query=hyp_vector,
        limit=top_k,
        with_payload=True,
    )
    return results.points

# Why does this work?
# Short queries: "What is the capital of France?"
# → Embeds in "question" space
# Hypothetical answer: "France is a European country. Its capital city is Paris..."
# → Embeds in "document" space — much closer to real documents!
Python — Re-ranking with Cohere
import cohere

co = cohere.Client("your-api-key")

def retrieve_and_rerank(question: str, top_k: int = 5):
    # Step 1: Over-fetch candidates (top 20 instead of top 5)
    # First-pass retrieval is fast but imprecise
    candidates = retrieve(question, top_k=20)
    candidate_texts = [c.payload["text"] for c in candidates]

    # Step 2: Re-rank with a cross-encoder model
    # Cross-encoder looks at (query, document) pairs together
    # Much more accurate than bi-encoder similarity
    rerank_result = co.rerank(
        query=question,
        documents=candidate_texts,
        top_n=top_k,
        model="rerank-v3.5",
    )

    # Step 3: Return the top-k re-ranked documents
    reranked = [
        {
            "text": candidate_texts[r.index],
            "relevance_score": r.relevance_score,
        }
        for r in rerank_result.results
    ]
    return reranked

Decision Guide

RAG vs Fine-Tuning — When to Use Which?

This is one of the most common questions in applied AI. The short answer: they solve different problems. Use the interactive decision helper below, then study the comparison table.

Do you need the model to access dynamic, frequently-updated information?
Dimension RAG Fine-Tuning
Knowledge Update Just update the vector DB — no retraining Requires full or partial retraining
Cost to Update Low — re-embed changed documents only High — GPU compute for training
Knowledge Type Factual, searchable, document-based Style, format, reasoning patterns, tone
Transparency High — can cite source documents Low — knowledge baked into weights
Hallucinations Reduced (grounded in retrieved context) Can still hallucinate confidently
Latency Adds retrieval step (~50-200ms) No extra step at inference time
Data Privacy Data stays in your DB, not in model Training data influences model weights
Long-Tail Queries Good — if doc exists, it can be retrieved Depends on training data coverage
Best Use Case Q&A over docs, support bots, research tools Custom tone, domain terminology, tasks

The Power Move: Use Both

The best production systems often combine RAG + fine-tuning. Fine-tune the model to understand your domain's vocabulary and preferred output format, then attach RAG to keep it grounded in current facts. Example: fine-tune a model on customer service conversations so it adopts the right tone, then add RAG over your product documentation so it always gives accurate, up-to-date answers.

Wrap-Up

What You've Learned

You've covered the entire RAG stack — from raw documents to grounded AI answers.

  • 1
    RAG was invented to solve three problems with base LLMs: knowledge cutoffs, hallucinations, and inability to access private data. It works in two phases: offline indexing and real-time querying.
  • 2
    Chunking splits documents into searchable pieces. The best default is recursive character splitting. Chunk size and overlap are critical hyperparameters that directly impact retrieval quality.
  • 3
    Embedding models convert text into dense vectors. Choose based on your dimension/cost/accuracy trade-off. Always use the same model at index time and query time. BGE-M3 and GTE are top open-source options; OpenAI text-embedding-3 is the leading commercial choice.
  • 4
    Vector databases store embeddings and enable fast ANN search. HNSW is the dominant indexing algorithm, offering the best speed/recall balance. Popular options: Qdrant, Pinecone, Weaviate, ChromaDB, and pgvector for PostgreSQL users.
  • 5
    Dense retrieval (cosine similarity) handles semantic queries. Sparse retrieval (BM25) handles exact keyword queries. Hybrid + RRF fusion gives the best of both. HyDE, multi-query, and re-ranking are advanced techniques that push accuracy further.
