Vision Models & Computer Vision

How machines learn to see — from basic pixel filters to models that understand the world as well as humans.

What is Computer Vision?

Teaching a computer to "see" and understand images and videos, just like your eyes and brain do automatically.

👁️
Analogy: Imagine you're a baby. When you first open your eyes, you see blurry blobs of color. Over years of looking at faces, dogs, cars and trees, your brain learned to recognize them. Computer Vision does the same thing — but instead of years, it trains on millions of images in hours. A computer "sees" an image as a grid of numbers (pixels), where each number represents how bright or what color that tiny dot is.
🖼️
Images are just numbers
A 224×224 RGB image is really a 3D array of 224×224×3 = 150,528 numbers. Each number (0–255) tells how red, green, or blue a pixel is. The computer never "sees" the picture — it only processes these numbers.
🎯
What CV can do
Classify images (cat or dog?), detect objects (draw boxes around all cars), segment scenes (color every pixel as sky/road/person), estimate depth, generate new images, read text in photos, and much more.
📱
Where you see it daily
Face unlock on your phone, photo apps that group friends, self-driving car cameras, medical X-ray analysis, Instagram filters, Google Lens, factory quality inspection.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

The workhorse of computer vision — a neural network architecture designed specifically to process images efficiently.

🔍
Analogy: Imagine sliding a small magnifying glass (called a filter or kernel) across a photo. Each position the magnifying glass stops at, it looks for a specific pattern — like an edge, a curve, or a color gradient. Early layers find simple things (edges, corners). Later layers combine those to find complex things (eyes → faces → people).

The CNN pipeline takes your image and transforms it step by step into a prediction.

Key CNN Concept: Feature Maps
When a filter slides over an image, it produces a new grid of numbers called a feature map. If the filter is looking for vertical edges, the feature map will "light up" wherever vertical edges exist in the image. A typical CNN layer has 64, 128, or 256 different filters — each looking for something different. All their feature maps are stacked together.
🌐
Convolutional Layer
Applies multiple small filters (e.g., 3×3) to the input. Each filter slides across the image computing dot products — finding patterns like edges, curves, textures. Output is a set of feature maps. Parameters = filter weights (learned during training).
🗜️
Pooling Layer
Max pooling takes the maximum value in each small region (e.g., 2×2), shrinking the feature map by half. This makes the network translation invariant — it doesn't matter if the cat moved 2 pixels left, pooling still produces similar outputs.
⚡
Activation (ReLU)
After each conv layer, we apply ReLU: f(x) = max(0, x). Negative values become zero. This adds non-linearity, letting the network learn complex patterns. Without it, stacking layers would still only solve linear problems.
🔗
Fully Connected Layer
At the end, the feature maps are flattened into a 1D vector and passed through regular neural network layers. This is where the final decision happens: "72% cat, 20% dog, 8% fox". The last layer uses Softmax to produce probabilities.

Famous CNN Architectures

1998
LeNet-5
Yann LeCun's pioneering CNN for handwritten digit recognition. 2 conv layers + 3 FC layers. Proved CNNs work. Used to read checks at US banks.
2012
AlexNet — The Deep Learning Revolution
Won ImageNet by a huge margin (15.3% vs 26.2% error). 5 conv layers, 3 FC layers, trained on GPUs. Sparked the modern deep learning era. Introduced ReLU and dropout.
2014
VGG-16 / GoogLeNet (Inception)
VGG showed depth matters — 16 layers with tiny 3×3 filters. GoogLeNet introduced "inception modules" — parallel filters of different sizes, processed simultaneously.
2015
ResNet — Residual Networks
Added "skip connections" — data can jump over layers. This solved the vanishing gradient problem, enabling 152-layer networks. ResNet-50 remains widely used today for transfer learning.
2021+
EfficientNet, ConvNeXt
EfficientNet scales width, depth, and resolution together via neural architecture search. ConvNeXt modernized CNN design using transformer design choices — competitive with ViT while being simpler.

Object Detection

Not just "what is in this image?" but "where are all the objects and what are they?" — drawing bounding boxes around every detected object.

📦
Analogy: Imagine looking at a busy street photo. Classification says "there are cars, people, and buildings." Detection says "there's a red car at coordinates (142, 230) that's 80 pixels wide and 60 pixels tall, a person at (300, 150)..." — it locates every object.
YOLO — You Only Look Once

Created by Joseph Redmon in 2015. The key insight: instead of looking at the image multiple times, look once and predict everything in one shot.

1

Divide into Grid

The image is divided into an S×S grid (e.g., 13×13 or 19×19 cells). Each cell is responsible for detecting objects whose center falls within it.

2

Predict Bounding Boxes + Confidence

Each cell predicts B bounding boxes (x, y, width, height) and a confidence score = P(object) × IoU. IoU (Intersection over Union) measures how well the predicted box matches the real one.

3

Predict Class Probabilities

Each cell also predicts C class probabilities (e.g., P(car), P(person), P(dog)...). Multiply by confidence to get final class-specific scores.

4

Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS)

Multiple cells may detect the same object. NMS keeps only the highest-confidence box per object and removes overlapping duplicates.

YOLO Versions
YOLOv1 (2015) → YOLOv3 (anchor boxes) → YOLOv5 (PyTorch, popular) → YOLOv8 (2023, Ultralytics) → YOLOv10/v11 (2024, anchor-free, NMS-free). Each version improved speed and accuracy. YOLOv8 can run at 80 FPS on an RTX 3080 — real-time video detection!
Faster R-CNN — Region-Based Detection

Ross Girshick's series (R-CNN → Fast R-CNN → Faster R-CNN) uses a two-stage approach: first find where objects might be, then classify them.

1

Backbone CNN

A pre-trained CNN (e.g., ResNet-50) extracts feature maps from the whole image. This shared computation makes everything faster.

2

Region Proposal Network (RPN)

A small neural network slides over the feature map and proposes ~300 "regions of interest" (RoIs) where objects might be. This replaced slow external methods like Selective Search.

3

RoI Pooling

Each proposed region is resized to the same fixed size (e.g., 7×7) using RoI Pooling, regardless of its original shape. This enables batch processing.

4

Classification & Refinement

Each RoI is classified (what object is it?) and its bounding box is refined (exactly where is it?). Two parallel outputs: class label + box coordinates.

Evolution to DETR
Facebook AI's DETR (2020) used the Transformer architecture for detection — no anchors, no NMS. It views detection as a "set prediction" problem. Grounding DINO (2023) combined DETR with language for open-vocabulary detection — detect anything you describe in text!

Image Segmentation

Going beyond bounding boxes — labeling every single pixel in an image with what it belongs to.

🎨
Semantic Segmentation
Every pixel gets a class label. All cars = blue, all people = red, all road = gray. But two cars get the same color — we don't distinguish individuals. Used in: autonomous driving (classify every pixel as road/sky/pedestrian).

Key model: FCN (Fully Convolutional Network), DeepLab v3+, SegFormer.
👥
Instance Segmentation
Like semantic but distinguishes individuals. Car #1 = dark blue, Car #2 = light blue. Combines detection (find objects) + segmentation (mask each one). Used in: medical imaging (count individual cells), robotics (which apple to pick).

Key model: Mask R-CNN (adds a mask head to Faster R-CNN), YOLOv8-seg.
🎭
Panoptic Segmentation
The best of both worlds — semantic (background classes like sky, road) + instance (countable objects like cars, people). Every pixel is labeled and every distinct object is identified separately.

Key model: Panoptic FPN, Mask2Former (2021 — unified architecture).
✂️
SAM — Segment Anything Model
Meta AI (2023) trained SAM on 1 billion masks. You can click on any object (or give a bounding box or text prompt) and SAM instantly segments it — with zero task-specific training. SAM 2 (2024) extends this to video.

Vision Transformers (ViT)

In 2020, Google Brain asked: "What if we applied the Transformer (designed for text) to images?" The answer changed everything.

🧩
Analogy: CNNs look at images through a sliding window — zooming into small patches. ViT does something different: it chops the image into 16×16 pixel squares (patches), treats each patch like a word, and processes all patches simultaneously — allowing each patch to "attend" to every other patch across the entire image at once.
1

Split into Patches

A 224×224 image is divided into 14×14 = 196 patches of size 16×16. Each patch is flattened into a 768-dimensional vector.

2

Linear Embedding

Each patch vector is projected into the model's embedding space via a linear layer (like word embeddings in text transformers).

3

Add Positional Embedding + [CLS] Token

Since transformers don't have built-in order awareness, we add positional embeddings. A special [CLS] token prepended learns to summarize the whole image.

4

Transformer Encoder

All 197 tokens (196 patches + [CLS]) go through Multi-Head Self-Attention layers. Every patch can attend to every other patch — no locality bias. The network learns global relationships.

5

Classification Head

The [CLS] token's final representation is passed through an MLP to predict the class. ViT needs large datasets to match CNN performance, so DeiT (2021) added distillation to train on smaller data.

ViT vs CNN Trade-offs
ViT advantages: Global attention (sees whole image at once), scales better with data and model size, versatile (works for detection, segmentation, generation with modifications). CNN advantages: Built-in translation invariance (pooling), works well on small datasets, faster for smaller images. Winner? Modern hybrid models (ConvNeXt, Swin Transformer) combine both — taking the best of each.

Advanced: CLIP, DALL-E & Stable Diffusion

The frontier where vision meets language — models that understand and generate images from text descriptions.

🔗 CLIP — Connecting Images and Language (OpenAI, 2021)
🔗
Analogy: CLIP learns by looking at 400 million (image, caption) pairs from the internet. It trains a vision model and a text model to produce similar embeddings for matching pairs, and different embeddings for mismatched ones. After training, you can compare any image to any text without retraining.

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) uses contrastive learning: given a batch of N image-text pairs, it trains the models so that the dot product of matching pairs is maximized and mismatched pairs is minimized. This creates a shared embedding space where "a photo of a cat" and a cat photo have similar vectors.

Zero-shot classification: To classify a new image, compare its embedding to text embeddings like "a photo of a cat", "a photo of a dog", etc. The highest dot product wins — no fine-tuning needed! CLIP matched ResNet-50 accuracy on ImageNet without seeing a single labeled ImageNet image.

Why CLIP Matters
CLIP is the visual backbone of most modern VLMs (LLaVA, GPT-4V, etc.). It's the "translation layer" between images and language. Almost every image generation model uses a CLIP-based text encoder to understand your text prompt.
🎨 Stable Diffusion — Generating Images from Text
✏️
Analogy: Imagine you have a perfect photo. Now imagine randomly erasing tiny portions until it's pure static/noise — like a TV with no signal. A diffusion model learns to reverse this process: starting from noise, it gradually "erases the noise" step by step until a coherent image emerges. The text prompt guides which image appears.
1

Forward Process (Training)

Gaussian noise is gradually added to training images over T steps (e.g., T=1000). The model learns to predict the noise added at each step.

2

Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion's trick)

Instead of diffusing in pixel space (expensive!), SD first encodes images into a compressed latent space via a VAE (Variational Autoencoder), then diffuses there. 8× smaller — much faster!

3

Text Conditioning via CLIP

Your prompt ("a cat wearing a hat, oil painting") is encoded by CLIP's text encoder into a vector. The U-Net denoiser uses cross-attention to condition each denoising step on this text vector.

4

Reverse Process (Generation)

Start with random noise in latent space. Run the U-Net ~20–50 times, each time removing a bit of noise guided by your text. Decode the final latent back to pixels via VAE. Done!

Key variants: SDXL (higher resolution), SD3 (flow matching, better text rendering), DALL-E 3 (OpenAI, integrated with ChatGPT), Midjourney (closed, artistic quality), Flux.1 (Black Forest Labs, 2024 — state of the art open source).

🏗️ DALL-E 3 — Text-to-Image via Diffusion Transformers

DALL-E 3 (OpenAI, 2023) significantly improved prompt following by training on synthetic, highly detailed image captions generated by GPT-4. The key insight: existing datasets have poor captions ("a dog in a park") — DALL-E 3 generated detailed captions like "A golden retriever sitting on green grass in Central Park, looking left, afternoon sunlight, photorealistic."

It uses a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) backbone instead of U-Net — applying transformer architecture to the diffusion process itself. This scales better and handles text rendering more accurately. Integrated directly into ChatGPT for natural language-guided image creation.

 Topic 2

Large Language Models (LLMs)

The technology behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini — models that read, write, reason, and converse in human language.

What is a Large Language Model?

🔮
Analogy: Imagine the world's most advanced autocomplete. Your phone predicts the next word when you type. An LLM predicts the next word too — but it has read practically the entire internet, understands context, can reason, write code, translate languages, and follow complex instructions. It has billions of parameters (like billions of knobs that got tuned during training) that encode patterns from all that text.
📏
How "Large"?
GPT-3 had 175 billion parameters. GPT-4 is estimated at ~1.8 trillion (MoE). DeepSeek-V3 has 671B total (37B active via MoE). A "parameter" is a number learned during training — think of it as the model's memory of patterns in language.
📚
What they're trained on
The whole internet (Common Crawl), Wikipedia, books (BookCorpus), code (GitHub), scientific papers, news articles — trillions of words. They learn grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, coding syntax, math, and much more purely from predicting the next word.
⚡
Emergent Capabilities
At a certain scale, new abilities "emerge" that weren't explicitly trained — like chain-of-thought reasoning, few-shot learning (learning from examples in the prompt), and multi-step arithmetic. Smaller models can't do these; larger ones suddenly can.

Tokenization — Breaking Language into Pieces

Before an LLM can process text, it must convert it into numbers. This is done via tokenization.

🧱
Analogy: LEGO bricks. Words get broken into sub-word pieces (tokens). "unhappiness" → ["un", "happi", "ness"]. Common words like "the", "is" become one token. Rare words get split into pieces. Numbers are often split digit by digit. Each token gets a unique ID number that the model processes.

🔤
BPE — Byte Pair Encoding
The most common tokenization algorithm. Start with individual characters. Merge the most frequent pair repeatedly until you have a vocabulary of ~50,000 tokens. Used by GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA. Balances vocabulary size vs. sequence length.
🌍
Multilingual Challenges
English text is ~4 chars/token. Chinese characters often need 2-3 tokens each. This means processing Chinese text is 2-3x more expensive and may hit context limits sooner. Newer tokenizers (like Qwen's) are optimized for Chinese with far better token efficiency.
🔢
Context Window
LLMs can only process a limited number of tokens at once — this is the "context window". GPT-4: 128K tokens. Claude 3: 200K. Gemini 1.5: 1 million. Think of it as working memory — the model forgets anything outside the window.

Transformer Architecture — The Engine of Modern AI

Introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" — the architecture that revolutionized AI and powers almost every modern LLM.

🎯
Analogy: When you read "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" — what does "it" refer to? You instantly know it's the trophy. Your brain automatically pays attention to the relevant earlier word. Transformers do this with their "self-attention" mechanism — each word simultaneously asks "which other words in this sentence should I pay attention to?"

Self-Attention: Q, K, V

📚
Analogy: Imagine a library. Your Query (Q) is what you're looking for. Each book has a Key (K) on its spine. The library searches for keys matching your query. Each book's content is its Value (V). Self-attention = every word searching the library of all other words simultaneously.
1

Create Q, K, V vectors

Each input token embedding is linearly projected three times to create Query, Key, and Value vectors (via learned weight matrices W_Q, W_K, W_V).

2

Compute Attention Scores

Score = Q · K^T (dot product). For each query, compute its similarity to all keys. Scale by √d_k to prevent vanishing gradients with large dimensions.

3

Softmax → Attention Weights

Apply softmax to scores to get probabilities (must sum to 1). High score = "pay more attention to this token". These are the attention weights.

4

Weighted Sum of Values

Multiply each Value vector by its attention weight, then sum. This gives a new representation for each token — a blend of all values, weighted by relevance. Formula: Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k) × V

Positional Encoding — Giving Order to Words

Unlike RNNs that process words one by one (inherently ordered), Transformers process all words in parallel — so they have no built-in sense of word order. "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog" would look the same without positional information!

📍
Original: Sinusoidal Encoding
Add a fixed sine/cosine wave pattern to each token's embedding based on its position. Position 1 gets one pattern, position 2 gets another. The model learns to read position from these signals. Advantage: can extrapolate to longer sequences than seen during training.
🔄
Modern: Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE)
Used by LLaMA, Qwen, Mistral. Instead of adding position info, rotate the Q and K vectors by an angle proportional to their position. The dot product between Q and K naturally encodes relative distance. Better at generalizing to longer contexts.
📐
ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases)
Add a linear bias to attention scores based on the distance between tokens. Closer tokens get higher scores. Very simple and effective for length generalization. Used by BLOOM, MPT.

Full Transformer Architecture

1

Token Embedding Layer

Convert token IDs (integers) into dense vectors of size d_model (e.g., 768 for GPT-2, 12,288 for GPT-4). These vectors are learned and represent meaning.

2

+ Positional Encoding

Add positional information to each embedding so the model knows where each token sits in the sequence.

3

N × Transformer Blocks

Repeat N times (e.g., GPT-2: 12, GPT-3: 96, LLaMA-70B: 80 layers). Each block:
① Multi-Head Self-Attention → ② Add & LayerNorm → ③ Feed-Forward Network (FFN) → ④ Add & LayerNorm.

4

Feed-Forward Network (FFN)

After attention, each token passes through a 2-layer MLP independently. Typically expands 4× then contracts: d_model → 4×d_model → d_model. This is where most "knowledge storage" happens — ~70% of parameters are in FFN layers.

5

Language Model Head

The final layer outputs logits (unnormalized scores) for every token in the vocabulary (~50,000 tokens). Softmax gives probabilities. We pick the next token via sampling or greedy decoding.

Multi-Head Attention — Parallel Perspectives

👀
Analogy: Instead of one person reading the text, imagine 12 people reading simultaneously, each focused on something different — one tracks grammar, one tracks topic, one tracks who is speaking, one tracks sentiment. Then they combine their findings. That's Multi-Head Attention.

Instead of one Q/K/V transformation, we have h=8 or 16 attention "heads" running in parallel, each with their own learned W_Q, W_K, W_V. Each head can specialize in different types of relationships. Their outputs are concatenated and linearly projected back to d_model.

Modern Variants
Grouped Query Attention (GQA): Used by LLaMA 2, Mistral, Qwen. Instead of one K/V per head, share K/V across groups of heads. Reduces memory by 4-8× with minimal quality loss — crucial for inference speed. Multi-Query Attention (MQA): Single K/V shared across ALL heads. Even more memory efficient.
Python (simplified attention)
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def self_attention(Q, K, V, d_k):
    # Q, K, V: (batch, seq_len, d_k)
    
    # Step 1: Compute attention scores
    scores = torch.matmul(Q, K.transpose(-2, -1))  # (batch, seq, seq)
    scores = scores / (d_k ** 0.5)  # Scale by sqrt(d_k)
    
    # Step 2: Apply causal mask (for autoregressive LLMs)
    # Prevents attending to future tokens
    mask = torch.triu(torch.ones_like(scores), diagonal=1).bool()
    scores = scores.masked_fill(mask, float('-inf'))
    
    # Step 3: Softmax → attention weights
    weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)  # (batch, seq, seq)
    
    # Step 4: Weighted sum of values
    output = torch.matmul(weights, V)  # (batch, seq, d_k)
    return output, weights

Pre-training, Fine-tuning & Alignment

🌐
Phase 1: Pre-training
Train on trillions of tokens from the internet. Objective: predict the next token. This is self-supervised — no human labels needed. Teaches language, facts, reasoning, coding, and math. Takes months on thousands of GPUs costing millions of dollars. Produces a "base model."
🎯
Phase 2: Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT)
Train on high-quality (prompt, ideal-response) pairs written by humans. Teaches the model to follow instructions and respond in the desired style. Much smaller dataset (~100K examples) but very carefully curated. Turns the base model into an "assistant model."
💡
Transfer Learning
You don't need to train from scratch for every task. Take a pre-trained model and fine-tune only on your task's data. Works because pre-training gives the model general knowledge. Even training just the last few layers often works. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) makes fine-tuning efficient by training tiny adapter matrices.
📏
Scaling Laws
Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020) showed that LLM performance improves predictably with: more parameters, more data, and more compute — following power laws. Chinchilla (2022) showed optimal training: use ~20 tokens per parameter (e.g., 7B model → train on 140B tokens).

RLHF — Making AI Helpful and Safe

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback teaches the model to be helpful, harmless, and honest — using human preferences as the training signal.

👨‍🏫
Analogy: Training a student (the LLM) using a strict teacher (reward model) who grades every response. The student tries different answers, the teacher rates them, and the student gradually learns to produce answers the teacher rates highly. The teacher's ratings were themselves trained on thousands of human-written comparisons.
1

Collect Comparison Data

Human annotators are shown the same prompt with 2-4 different model responses. They rank them from best to worst. Thousands of these comparisons are collected.

2

Train a Reward Model (RM)

A separate model is fine-tuned to predict human preference scores from these comparisons. Given a prompt + response, it outputs a single "quality score." This RM learns what humans consider helpful, accurate, and safe.

3

Fine-tune with PPO

Use Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) — an RL algorithm — to update the LLM's weights. The LLM generates responses, the RM scores them, and PPO updates the LLM to produce higher-scoring responses. A KL-divergence penalty prevents the model from drifting too far from the SFT baseline.

4

Modern Alternative: DPO & GRPO

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO, 2023) skips training a separate reward model — it directly fine-tunes on (preferred, rejected) pairs with a clever mathematical reformulation. Simpler, more stable. GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) used by DeepSeek-R1 trains reasoning without RLHF by using outcome verification.

Python — Fine-tuning with LoRA (using PEFT)
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model

# Load a base model
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf")
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf")

# Configure LoRA — only train tiny adapter matrices
lora_config = LoraConfig(
    r=16,                    # Rank — smaller = fewer params
    lora_alpha=32,           # Scaling factor

# Wrap model with LoRA — reduces trainable params by ~100x!

model.print_trainable_parameters()

Advanced LLM Techniques

🔍 RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation
📖
Analogy: Open-book exam vs closed-book. LLMs without RAG are closed-book — they rely only on memorized knowledge. RAG lets the model open a book (search a knowledge base) before answering. Much better for up-to-date facts, private data, and reducing hallucinations.
1

Index Documents

Break your documents into chunks. Embed each chunk with an embedding model (e.g., text-embedding-3-small). Store in a vector database (Pinecone, Weaviate, ChromaDB).

2

Retrieve at Query Time

Embed the user's question. Find the top-k most similar chunks via cosine similarity or ANN search. These are the "relevant passages."

3

Generate with Context

Stuff retrieved passages into the prompt: "Given this context: [passages], answer: [question]". The LLM reads the retrieved info and generates an answer grounded in it.

🧠 Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Reasoning

Adding "Let's think step by step" to a prompt dramatically improves performance on math, logic, and multi-step problems. The model writes out intermediate reasoning steps instead of jumping to the answer.

💭
Zero-shot CoT
Just add "Let's think step by step" to any question. Surprisingly powerful — nearly doubles performance on GSM8K (math word problems) with GPT-3.5.
📝
Few-shot CoT
Provide examples with reasoning chains in the prompt. The model follows the pattern and produces its own reasoning chain. Shows it how to "think" on a specific task type.
🔮
o1/R1 Style — RL for Reasoning
OpenAI o1, DeepSeek-R1 train models to produce long reasoning chains using RL. No human-labeled reasoning needed — the model discovers reasoning strategies via reward for getting correct answers.
✍️ Prompt Engineering — Getting the Best Out of LLMs
🎭
Role Prompting
"You are an expert cardiologist..." Giving the model a role improves domain-specific accuracy and sets the response style.
🧩
Few-shot Examples
Show 3-5 examples of input→output format before your actual question. The model learns the pattern instantly — no fine-tuning needed.
🔧
Structured Output
"Return as JSON with fields: name, age, city." LLMs can reliably produce structured data — crucial for integrating AI into applications.
🔄
Self-Consistency
Generate the same question multiple times with temperature>0, then take a majority vote over answers. Reduces reasoning errors significantly for complex problems.

OCR — Optical Character Recognition

Teaching machines to read text in images — from handwritten letters to complex document layouts.

What is OCR and Its History

📜
Analogy: When you take a photo of a book page, your eyes instantly read the text — your brain recognizes letter shapes. OCR is teaching a computer to do the same: take an image of text (a scanned document, photo of a sign, screenshot), and output the actual text characters. The challenge: text comes in thousands of fonts, handwriting styles, languages, rotations, lighting conditions, and backgrounds.
1914-1950s
Early Machines
Emanuel Goldberg built the first character recognition machine in 1914. By 1951, David Shepard's "Gismo" could read typewritten characters. These used physical template matching — a very limited set of fonts.
1970s-80s
Commercial OCR

Deep Learning OCR — CRNN Architecture

🔤
Analogy: CRNN is like having two specialists working together: a CNN that looks at the image and extracts visual features column by column (left to right), then an LSTM that reads those features in sequence and says "that's an H, then an e, then l, l, o." The two work in perfect tandem — vision then sequence reading.
1

CNN — Visual Feature Extraction

A CNN processes the input image (e.g., 32×W pixels, where W varies by text length). Multiple convolutional layers extract features. The result is a feature map of shape (H=1, W_reduced, C=512) — essentially a sequence of feature vectors, one per image column.

2

Bidirectional LSTM — Sequence Reading

The feature sequence is fed to stacked BiLSTM layers that process left-to-right AND right-to-left simultaneously. Captures context: "is this a 'c' or an 'e'?" depends on neighboring characters.

3

CTC Loss — No Alignment Needed

Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) handles the alignment problem: the image has W columns but the text has N characters (W ≠ N). CTC allows the model to output "blank" tokens and repeat predictions, then collapses them. You don't need to annotate which pixel corresponds to which letter!

4

Modern: Attention-based & TrOCR

Replace CTC with a Transformer decoder + attention. TrOCR (Microsoft, 2021) uses a ViT encoder to process the image as patches, then a BERT-style decoder to generate characters autoregressively. Achieves state-of-the-art on handwriting recognition.

Vision-Language Models (VLMs)

Where eyes meet words — AI systems that simultaneously understand images and language, enabling them to answer questions about pictures, describe scenes, and reason visually.

What are Vision-Language Models?

🧠
Analogy: Imagine a person who can both see and read fluently. When you hand them a photo and ask "Is there a dog in this picture?" they use both their visual system (eyes) AND their language system (brain's language area) simultaneously. 💬

📄
Document AI
Understand entire documents as images — invoices, contracts, reports, scientific papers. Extract structured info, answer questions about content, summarize multi-page PDFs. GPT-4V and Qwen-VL excel here.
🎭
Grounded Understanding
Not just describing but localizing — "Draw a box around the stop sign." GPT-4V can output bounding box coordinates. Grounding DINO + CLIP enables open-vocabulary detection via natural language.

Key Vision-Language Models

GPT-4V & GPT-4o — OpenAI's Multimodal Models

OpenAI extended GPT-4 with vision capabilities (GPT-4V, 2023) and then natively trained GPT-4o as a multimodal model from the ground up (2024). Unlike GPT-4V which processes image and text separately, GPT-4o was trained end-to-end on all modalities simultaneously.

Capabilities
Document analysis: Read invoices, contracts, research papers. Chart understanding: Interpret graphs and data visualizations. Code from screenshots: Write code that reproduces a UI shown in a screenshot. Medical imaging: Describe X-rays, MRIs. Mathematics: Solve problems shown in handwritten or typeset images.

How it works: GPT-4V uses a CLIP-based vision encoder to embed the image, then interleaves visual tokens with text tokens fed to the GPT-4 transformer. High-resolution images are tiled and processed in segments, with results merged.

LLaVA — Large Language and Vision Assistant

LLaVA (Haotian Liu et al., 2023) is the most influential open-source VLM architecture. Its elegant design made multimodal AI accessible to researchers worldwide. Architecture: CLIP ViT-L/14 (vision) + a simple linear projection + LLaMA/Vicuna (language).

1

Stage 1: Feature Alignment (Projection pre-training)

Train ONLY the projection layer (MLP) on 558K image-text pairs (CC3M dataset). Both CLIP and LLaMA are frozen. This aligns visual features to the LLaMA embedding space.

2

Stage 2: End-to-End Fine-tuning

Unfreeze LLaMA, keep CLIP frozen. Fine-tune on 158K GPT-4 generated visual instruction pairs. Produces a model that can follow diverse visual instructions.

LLaVA Family
LLaVA-1.5 (2023): improved connector (MLP instead of linear), better data curation. LLaVA-NeXT/OneVision (2024): higher resolution, multi-image, video understanding. LLaVA-Med: fine-tuned for medical imaging. Countless open-source applications built on LLaVA.
Flamingo — DeepMind's Few-Shot Visual Learner

Flamingo (Alayrac et al., DeepMind, 2022) pioneered in-context learning for multimodal AI. Unlike LLaVA's simple MLP connector, Flamingo uses a sophisticated Perceiver Resampler and Gated Cross-Attention layers.

Perceiver Resampler
Takes variable-length image features from a large vision encoder and compresses them into a fixed number of visual tokens (64 or 256). Uses learned latent queries that "sample" the most relevant visual information via cross-attention.
Gated Cross-Attention
Inserted between frozen LLM layers. Text tokens attend to visual tokens via cross-attention. A learned "tanh gate" controls how much visual information flows through — starts at 0, grows during training.

Key advantage: Flamingo enables interleaved image-text prompts: "Image1: [cat photo] This is a cat. Image2: [dog photo] This is a dog. Image3: [rabbit photo] What is this?" — few-shot visual learning!

Gemini — Google's Native Multimodal Model

Gemini (Google DeepMind, 2023) was designed multimodal from the ground up — not text-first with vision bolted on. It natively processes text, images, audio, and video simultaneously.

Gemini 1.5 Pro Capabilities
1 million token context window — can process an entire codebase or 1 hour of video. Native multimodal understanding (not just vision + text, but audio too). Gemini 1.5 Flash runs fast and cheap with 1M context. Used in Google Workspace, Search, and Lens.

Key innovations: Efficient attention (linear attention approximations for long contexts), audio tokens processed natively alongside text and image tokens, able to follow instructions across modalities ("describe what's happening in this video at timestamp 2:30").

DeepSeek-R1 — Reasoning via Pure RL

R1's key insight: you don't need human-annotated reasoning chains (like OpenAI's o1). Instead, train with pure reinforcement learning using a simple reward: does the final answer match the correct answer?

1

Cold Start: SFT on Chain-of-Thought

Fine-tune DeepSeek-V3 on a small set of long chain-of-thought examples to give the RL training a good starting point.

2

GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization

For each problem, sample G responses from the model. Score each response (correct = reward 1, wrong = 0). Update the model to prefer higher-scoring responses relative to the group average. No reward model needed!

3

Emergent "Thinking"

The model spontaneously develops reasoning behaviors: self-verification ("wait, let me reconsider"), breaking problems into steps, backtracking — all discovered through RL without being explicitly programmed.

4

Distillation to Smaller Models

R1's reasoning is distilled into smaller models (1.5B, 7B, 14B, 32B, 70B). Remarkably, the 7B distilled model beats GPT-4o on many math benchmarks. Open source, downloadable, runnable on consumer hardware.

🔧
Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA)
Compresses the K and V cache into low-dimensional latent vectors, reducing KV cache memory by 5-13× vs. standard MHA. Enables very long context at much lower memory cost.
🎯
Auxiliary-Loss-Free Load Balancing
MoE models have a problem: some experts get over-used. DeepSeek's method dynamically adjusts routing bias to balance expert load without adding a separate loss term — cleaner training.
⚡
FP8 Mixed Precision Training
Train with 8-bit floating point (FP8) instead of 16-bit. Cuts memory and communication costs in half. Required careful numerical stability solutions. Enabled fitting the full model on fewer chips.
🔮
Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)
Standard LLMs predict one next token at a time. DeepSeek-V3 predicts multiple future tokens in parallel with auxiliary heads, then uses this during training (not inference) to improve data efficiency.

Model Deployment & Compute Optimization

From trained weights to live inference — every trick engineers use to make LLMs fast, memory-efficient, and production-ready on any hardware.

The Deployment Problem

Training a model is only half the job. Running it fast enough to be useful — for millions of users, on a budget — is a completely different engineering challenge.

🍽️
Analogy: A trained model is like a master chef's recipe book — enormously valuable, but completely useless until you have a restaurant kitchen that can serve 1,000 customers per hour. The recipe doesn't change; what changes is the kitchen setup, the batch cooking tricks, and the staffing (compute) you can afford. Deployment engineering is kitchen design.

Every deployment engineer juggles three competing pressures:

⚡
Latency
Time for the model to return the first token. Critical for chat interfaces. Target: <200ms Time-To-First-Token (TTFT).
📊
Throughput
Tokens per second across all users at once. Critical for batch APIs. Higher = cheaper per token at scale.
🧠
Memory
GPU VRAM is the hard ceiling. Model weights + KV cache + activations must all fit. Overflow = crash or swap (very slow).
💸
Cost
GPU-hours are expensive. Optimization reduces cost per token from $0.01 to $0.0001. That 100× difference is a business model.

Two Phases of LLM Inference
Prefill — process all prompt tokens in parallel (like reading the whole question at once). This is compute-bound and fast.
Decode — generate output tokens one at a time, each requiring a full forward pass. This is memory-bandwidth-bound and is where most latency comes from. Everything in this section is about making decode faster and cheaper.

vLLM & PagedAttention

The most impactful LLM serving optimization of 2023 — inspired by how operating systems manage RAM with virtual memory paging.

💾
Analogy: Think of GPU memory like a hotel. The naive approach reserves a whole floor (say, 200 rooms) for each guest at check-in — even if they only ever use 10 rooms. PagedAttention is like a smart hotel manager who assigns rooms one-by-one as the guest actually needs them, and immediately reassigns rooms the moment a guest checks out. The result: 5× more guests can stay at the same time.

Why KV Cache Was Broken Before vLLM

🧩
What is the KV Cache?
During autoregressive decoding, every new token needs to "look at" all previous tokens via attention. Recomputing those Key and Value vectors from scratch every step would be insanely slow. The KV cache stores them on GPU so each step only computes the new token's K/V and reuses the rest.
💥
The Fragmentation Problem
Old frameworks (vanilla HuggingFace) pre-allocate a contiguous block of GPU memory equal to the maximum possible sequence length for every request in the batch — even if that request only ever generates 50 tokens. Result: 60–80% of KV cache memory is wasted on empty reservations.
✅
PagedAttention Solution
Inspired by OS virtual memory paging. KV cache is split into fixed-size blocks (e.g., 16 tokens each). Each request is given exactly as many blocks as it currently needs, with new blocks allocated one-at-a-time as tokens are generated. Memory waste drops to under 4%.

Prefix Caching — Reuse KV blocks across requests with identical prefixes

If 1,000 users all send requests starting with the same system prompt ("You are a helpful assistant..."), there's no reason to recompute its KV vectors 1,000 times. Prefix caching hashes the token sequence of any prefix and stores its KV blocks. The next request with an identical prefix reuses those cached blocks instantly — skipping the prefill computation entirely for that portion.

Impact
For RAG pipelines (long shared context), chatbots (shared system prompt), and coding assistants (shared file context), prefix caching can eliminate 70–90% of prefill compute. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google all offer this as a billed feature (called "prompt caching").
Chunked Prefill — Don't let big prompts starve small ones

Normally, a massive 32K-token prompt monopolizes the GPU for hundreds of milliseconds during prefill, stalling all other requests. Chunked prefill splits the prompt into smaller chunks (e.g., 512 tokens) processed over multiple iterations, interleaved with decode steps for other requests. This prevents head-of-line blocking and dramatically improves tail latency (P99 TTFT).

Continuous Batching

The scheduling innovation that makes GPU utilization go from 40% to 90%+ — by treating inference as a stream, not a fixed-size batch.

🚇
Analogy: Static batching is like a bus — it waits at the depot until all seats are filled, then drives the whole route. If some passengers need only 2 stops and others need 20, the short-trip passengers just sit idle on the bus for the long haul. Continuous batching is like a subway — it departs on schedule, passengers board at every station, and anyone who reaches their stop exits immediately while new riders board. The train stays full and moving.

How Iteration-Level Scheduling Works

1

Requests arrive and enter a waiting queue

New requests (with their full prompts) are added to a priority queue. The scheduler inspects available GPU memory (KV cache blocks) to decide how many can begin prefill right now.

2

Prefill step — process new request prompts

Selected requests run prefill in parallel (all prompt tokens processed at once, like a matrix multiply). Their KV blocks are allocated from the paged memory pool. This is compute-heavy but fast.

3

Decode step — all active requests generate one token

Every active request generates exactly one token. The batch is now heterogeneous: some requests are on token 5, others on token 500. The GPU processes them all in a single batched forward pass.

4

Finished requests exit immediately

The moment a request generates its EOS (end-of-sequence) token or hits max_tokens, it is ejected from the batch. Its KV cache blocks are freed instantly — no waiting for others to finish.

5

New requests fill the freed slots immediately

The scheduler sees newly freed KV blocks and immediately admits the next request from the waiting queue. There is no "wait for the whole batch to finish" — this is the key difference from static batching.

6

Preemption — swap out if memory gets full

If memory runs short, vLLM can preempt low-priority requests — swapping their KV cache to CPU RAM (or simply discarding and re-prefilling later). This prevents OOM crashes and keeps the GPU running.

Why This Matters in Practice
On a workload where average output length is 200 tokens but variance is high (10–2000 tokens), continuous batching improves GPU utilization from ~40% (static) to ~85–95%. This directly translates to 2–3× lower cost per token at the same latency. It's the core reason vLLM and TGI outperform vanilla HuggingFace by such large margins.

How GPTQ Works

GPTQ (Generative Pre-trained Transformer Quantization) quantizes weights layer by layer using the Optimal Brain Compression (OBC) framework. For each layer, it finds the weight values closest to the original in terms of output error (not just raw value proximity), using a small calibration dataset.

  • Quantizes to 4-bit (or 3/2-bit) integers
  • Uses calibration data (~128 samples) to minimize reconstruction error
  • Fast inference via ExLlamaV2 or AutoGPTQ backends
  • Asymmetric quantization: separate scale + zero-point per group
  • Group size 128 = good quality/speed tradeoff
Best For
GPU inference where you need high throughput and can spare 10–30 min for quantization. Popular for HuggingFace model releases.

How AWQ Works

AWQ (Activation-Aware Weight Quantization) makes a key observation: only ~1% of weights are "salient" (disproportionately important for output quality). Instead of carefully quantizing every weight equally (GPTQ), AWQ finds these important weights using activation magnitudes, then scales them up before quantization so they lose less relative precision.

  • No backpropagation or weight reconstruction needed
  • Much less sensitive to calibration data quality
  • Typically outperforms GPTQ at same bit-width
  • Supports W4A16 (weights 4-bit, activations 16-bit)
  • Excellent vLLM and TGI integration via AWQ kernels
Best For
Production GPU serving where quality matters most. The default choice for vLLM + 4-bit deployments in 2024–2025.

llama.cpp Deep Dive

The open-source project that democratized local LLM inference — running multi-billion parameter models on your laptop with zero cloud dependency.

🎻
Analogy: llama.cpp is like fitting a concert orchestra into a garage — and discovering it sounds surprisingly good. It strips away everything that requires expensive GPU infrastructure, hand-writes optimized CPU kernels in pure C++, and delivers real-time inference on hardware everyone already owns. The "orchestra" (LLM) doesn't change; the "venue" (compute) is radically different.
⚡
Why C++?
Python is slow for tight numerical loops. llama.cpp writes all hot-path math (matrix multiplication, attention, sampling) in hand-vectorized C++ with AVX2/AVX-512 SIMD intrinsics — squeezing every FLOP from CPU cache hierarchies and memory bandwidth.
📁
GGUF Format
GGUF (GGML Universal Format) is a single-file format storing everything needed: model architecture, hyperparameters, tokenizer vocab, and quantized weight tensors. One file, no config.json needed. Supports memory-mapped I/O so weights don't need to fully load into RAM.
🎮
GPU Offloading
The --n-gpu-layers flag offloads transformer layers to GPU. If your GPU has 8GB VRAM and the model needs 16GB, offload half the layers to GPU (fast) and run the other half on CPU (slower). Mixed-device inference beats pure CPU significantly.

How GPU Layer Offloading Works (n_gpu_layers)

A transformer has N identical layers (e.g., Llama 3 8B has 32 layers). Each layer is an independent tensor operation. When you set -ngl 20, layers 0–19 run on GPU and layers 20–31 run on CPU. The bottleneck is data transfer between CPU and GPU for the boundary layers — but since most compute is within each layer, the speedup is roughly proportional.

-ngl 0 (CPU only)
All computation on CPU. Slowest. 3–10 tokens/sec on modern laptop with Q4_K_M 7B.
-ngl 15 (half-half)
Split load. Useful when VRAM can't fit the whole model. 10–25 tokens/sec.
-ngl 99 (all GPU)
Maximum speed when VRAM fits. 40–80+ tokens/sec on RTX 3090 with Q4_K_M 7B.

CPU vs GPU Inference

Understanding when and why GPUs dominate — and the rare cases where CPU inference is actually the right choice.

🚗
Analogy: A GPU is a 20-lane highway — incredible throughput for massive parallel traffic, but has a minimum lane width. A CPU is a 16-lane freeway with flexible lanes — each lane is faster for a single car, better for sporadic low-volume traffic. LLM decoding (1 token at a time) is often a single-car problem, which is why GPUs underutilize their lanes and CPUs punch above their weight at low batch sizes.

The Roofline Model — Why LLM Decoding is Memory-Bound

Performance is limited either by memory bandwidth (how fast you can load weights) or peak compute (how fast you can multiply). LLM decode is almost always in the memory-bound zone.

The Critical Insight
LLM decode at batch size 1 has an arithmetic intensity of ~1–2 FLOPs per byte (you load billions of parameters just to do a handful of multiplies per token). An A100's ridge point is ~156 FLOPs/byte. This is why a fast CPU with high memory bandwidth (like Apple M2 Ultra at 800 GB/s) can be surprisingly competitive with a GPU for single-user inference.

Speculative Decoding

Getting 2–3× faster output from a large model — without changing its outputs — by letting a small model do most of the guessing.

✍️
Analogy: Imagine a student (draft model) who writes a rough paragraph in seconds, and a professor (verifier model) who reviews the whole paragraph in a single read — accepting correct sentences and rewriting wrong ones. Without speculation, the professor writes every sentence from scratch, one at a time. With speculation, they review 5 sentences per read instead of 1. Same final quality, 3× the speed.

Verifier Model

The target LLM
1

Draft model generates γ candidate tokens

The small draft model autoregressively generates γ (typically 4–8) candidate tokens. This is fast because the model is tiny. No constraint on what it predicts — it just guesses.

2

Verifier runs a single forward pass over all γ tokens

The big model processes all γ draft tokens in a single parallel forward pass (like prefill, not decode). It outputs the probability distribution for each position simultaneously. This costs roughly the same compute as generating just 1 token normally.

3

Accept or reject using rejection sampling

For each draft token, compute acceptance probability = min(1, p_target(x) / p_draft(x)). If accepted, move to the next. At the first rejection, stop. This guarantees the exact same distribution as the original large model — no quality loss.

4

Resample the rejected position

At the rejection point, the verifier samples a corrected token from an adjusted distribution. This correction keeps the output distribution identical to pure large model sampling.

5

Net gain: (accepted tokens + 1) per verifier pass

Average speedup = (E[accepted tokens] + 1) per verifier cost. When acceptance rate is high, you get 4–5 tokens per verifier pass instead of 1. Typical speedups: 2–3× on coding, 1.5× on open-ended generation.

Question 1 of 10 — Vision Models
What does a "filter" (kernel) do in a Convolutional Neural Network?
Correct! A convolutional filter (kernel) is a small learnable weight matrix that slides across the image. At each position, it computes the dot product between filter weights and the local image patch — producing one value in the output feature map. This detects specific patterns (edges, textures, curves) wherever they appear.
Question 2 of 10 — Vision Models
What is the main advantage of YOLO over Faster R-CNN for object detection?
Correct! YOLO (You Only Look Once) is a single-stage detector — it makes all predictions (bounding boxes + classes) in a single forward pass through the network. This makes it 5-10× faster than two-stage detectors like Faster R-CNN, enabling real-time detection (80+ FPS). The trade-off is slightly lower accuracy, especially on small objects (though this gap has closed in v8/v10).
Question 3 of 10 — Vision Models
How does Vision Transformer (ViT) process an image differently from a CNN?
Correct! ViT divides the image into fixed-size patches (e.g., 16×16 pixels), flattens each patch into a vector, and treats the sequence of patches as "tokens" — just like words in a Transformer. This allows each patch to attend to every other patch globally, unlike CNNs which only look at local regions through their sliding filter windows.
Question 4 of 10 — LLMs
In the self-attention mechanism, what do Q (Query), K (Key), and V (Value) represent?
Correct! Q, K, V are three linear projections of each token's embedding. The Query represents "what am I looking for?" — it scores against all Keys to find the most relevant tokens. The Values are the actual content that gets retrieved. The formula: Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k) × V. Think of Q as your search query, K as the index terms, and V as the documents themselves.
Question 5 of 10 — LLMs
What is the main purpose of RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) in training LLMs?
Correct! RLHF trains a reward model on human preference comparisons, then uses RL (typically PPO) to fine-tune the LLM to maximize this reward. It's how ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini become helpful assistants rather than raw text predictors. Without RLHF, LLMs might produce harmful, biased, or unhelpful responses even if they're very capable language models.
Question 6 of 10 — LLMs
What is tokenization and why is it needed before an LLM can process text?
Correct! LLMs (and all neural networks) only process numbers. Tokenization converts text into a sequence of integer IDs by splitting it into sub-word units called tokens. For example, "unhappiness" → ["un", "happi", "ness"] → [31383, 9581, 1885]. BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) is the most common algorithm, building a vocabulary of ~50K tokens that balances granularity and sequence length.
Question 7 of 10 — OCR
In the CRNN architecture for OCR, what problem does CTC (Connectionist Temporal Classification) solve?
Correct! The alignment problem in OCR: the image might be 200 columns wide but the word has only 5 characters. CRNN outputs a probability distribution over characters for each image column, producing a sequence longer than the actual text. CTC allows "blank" tokens and repeated characters, then collapses this to get the final text — without any pixel-to-character annotation in the training data!
Question 8 of 10 — OCR
Which OCR system is best known for Chinese text recognition and is used in production at massive scale?
Correct! PaddleOCR, developed by Baidu using the PaddlePaddle framework, is the industry-leading solution for Chinese OCR and one of the best overall OCR systems. It supports 80+ languages, handles complex Chinese layouts, seals, and watermarks, and is used in production at Baidu scale. RapidOCR is a popular lightweight port of PaddleOCR models that runs without PaddlePaddle.
Question 9 of 10 — VLMs
In LLaVA's architecture, what is the role of the "projection layer" (connector)?
Correct! The projection layer (a simple MLP in LLaVA-1.5) is the critical "bridge" between vision and language. CLIP produces visual features in its own embedding space (e.g., 1024-dimensional). The LLM expects token embeddings in its own space (e.g., 4096-dimensional for LLaMA-13B).
